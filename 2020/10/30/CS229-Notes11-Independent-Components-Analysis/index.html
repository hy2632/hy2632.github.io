Non-Gaussian

Gaussian’s Isotropy results in density rotationally symmetric.

If s∼N(0,I)s \sim N(0,I)s∼N(0,I)

Es[x]=E[As]=AE[s]=0E_s[x] = E[As] = AE[s] = 0 Es​[x]=E[As]=AE[s]=0

Cov[x]=E[xxT]−(E[x])2=E[xxT]=E[AssTAT]=A⋅Cov[s]⋅AT=AAT\begin{aligned}Cov[x] & = E[xx^T] - (E[x])^2 \\ & = E[xx^T]\\ & = E[Ass^TA^T]\\ & = A\cdot Cov[s] \cdot A^T\\ & = AA^T \end{aligned}Cov[x]​=E[xxT]−(E[x])2=E[xxT]=E[AssTAT]=A⋅Cov[s]⋅AT=AAT​

Basically,

x∼N(0,AAT)x \sim N(0, AA^T) x∼N(0,AAT)

Let RRR be an arbitrary orthogonal(e.g. rotation/reflection) matrix, A′=ARA' = ARA′=AR, then x′=A′sx' = A'sx′=A′s,

the distribution of x′x'x′ is also gaussian x′∼N(0,AAT)x'\sim N(0, AA^T)x′∼N(0,AAT) because

A′A′T=ARRTAT=AATA'A'^T = ARR^TA^T = AA^T A′A′T=ARRTAT=AAT

Then we could never figure out whether the mixing matrix is AAA or A′A'A′.

However, so long as sss is distributed anisotropically, we could recover the mixing matrix.(Imagine you have 4 rotation-symmetrical sources, the n rotating these sources simulatenously does not influence the volume)
